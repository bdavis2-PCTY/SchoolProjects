STATE CHANGES


Solid

Anything is able to change states of matter, as long as it has the required energy and pressure to do so. If the pressure doesn't meet the requirement for a certain state, then the object may just skip to the next state of matter. A perfect example of this is dry ice. Carbon dioxide requires about 5-times of Earth atmosphere to base able to become a liquid. Since Earth doesn't have that much pressure, carbon dioxide will go from a solid (dry ice) straight to a gas (carbon dioxide). All the states are the same: add energy and it will change to the next state, remove energy and it will go back to the previous state.

Most common methods

Melting: Solid to liquid

When a solid gains heat energy, it will normally melt into a liquid. When the molecules get more energy, they start to spread apart, making them still stay together but they are able to take the shape of their container.

Freezing: Liquid to solid

When an object cools down enough, it will loose energy, freezing it back into a liquid. Since there's energy lost, the molecules don't have energy to move around and take shape of their container, making it a fixed shape.



Evaporation: Liquid to gas

When a liquid gains even more heat energy, it will evaporate into a gas. With the added energy, the molecules can float around, taking the shape of their container or spreading out everywhere.

Condensation: Gas to liquid

When a gas looses energy, it will revert back into a liquid. Due to the lack of energy, the molecules are unable to float around, but they will still take the shape of their container or spread out.



Uncommon methods

Ionization: Gas to plasma

This is unlikely to happen on Earth. When enough energy is added to a gas, it will ionize into a plasma.

Deionization: Plasma to gas

Once plasma cools enough, it will revert back into the gas phase.



Sublimation: Solid to gas

This is what happens with dry-ice on Earth. If there isn't enough pressure, a solid will sublime straight into a gas without ever being a liquid.

Deposition: Gas to solid

This also occurs with dry-ice on Earth. When there isn't enough pressure, a gas will depose into a solid without being a liquid.
